Part 2: How to Play a Barre Chords

1. Getting your Finger Position Correct

When practicing barre chords, use the stronger part of your finger near the thumb, rather than the softer, squishy area. To get used to the pressure required, place your middle finger over your index finger. This will help you feel the amount of pressure needed to press the strings properly, and with practice, you'll develop the necessary muscle memory.

2. Press your Thumb against the back of the neck

Think of holding your fingers on the guitar neck as if you're squishing a bug. Apply pressure from both sides of the neck to achieve the clearest sound. It might feel awkward at first, but with practice, it will become more natural.

3. Begin a practice routine

Spend 10–15 minutes daily practicing just your index finger across the any fret. Pluck or strum each string to ensure you get a clear sound from each one.

4. Practice holding this position while moving between frets

Keep in mind that the farther you move from the neck, the lower the string tension becomes, making it slightly easier to press down and hold the barre.

5. Apply Chord Shapes

Focus on strengthening your index finger into a solid barre before adding other fingers to form full chords.

Set of Barre Chords to Practice

Focus on mastering these barre chords: F, B, Bm, and C#m. These chords will help you build strength and precision in your fretting hand. Practice holding the barre firmly and adding the remaining fingers to form each chord shape. Spend time transitioning between these chords to improve your fluency and build muscle memory.

Part 4: Finger Picking

Training your fingers

1. Position your hands over the strings of your guitar.

Before learning fingerpicking patterns, familiarize yourself with your hand's position over the strings. Rest your thumb on the sixth string, your index finger on the third string, your middle finger on the second string, and your ring finger on the first string.

2. Curve all segments of your fingers

Your fingers have three joints, and each should maintain a slight curve, as if you're holding an apple or tennis ball in your palm. Keep your wrist and fingers relaxed for better control and comfort.

3. Curl your finger towards your palm to pick a string.

For fingerpicking, move your fingers by swinging them toward your palm, using only the base knuckle for movement. Practice this motion with your thumb and the first three fingers individually.

4. Move your thumb from the base joint.

Your thumb, like your fingers, has 3 joints. You can feel the base joint near your wrist by bending your thumb inward toward your palm. This base joint provides the strength and power for your thumb's movement.

5. Try basic fingerpicking exercises.

Fingerpicking exercises help your fingers move independently and prepare you for patterns. Even simple strumming with focus on finger movement can be effective—you don’t need specialized exercises to get started.

For example, you can practice fingerpicking a G chord followed by a C chord. Play the lowest note of the chord with your thumb, the next lowest with your first finger, next with your second finger, and highest note with your third finger.

6. Fingerpicking with Chords

You’re now practicing fingerpicking with the chords G, Em, C, and D using the same fingerpicking pattern. The only adjustment is the position of your thumb:

  • G: The thumb plucks the 6th string.
  • Em: The thumb plucks the 5th string.
  • C: The thumb plucks the 5th string.
  • D: The thumb plucks the 4th string.

Part 7: Tips to Improve Your Guitar Playing (Intermediate Level)

  1. Master Barre Chords – Work on smooth transitions between barre chords. Focus on finger strength and precision.
  2. Practice Scales – Learn major, minor, and pentatonic scales to improve finger agility and soloing.
  3. Work on Timing – Use a metronome to improve rhythm and timing. Play along with backing tracks for real-world application.
  4. Learn Chord Inversions – Get comfortable with different positions of the same chord across the neck for more versatile playing.
  5. Focus on Fingerpicking – Incorporate fingerpicking into your practice to add complexity and variety to your playing.
  6. Experiment with Dynamics – Play softly and loudly to add emotion and variation to your songs.
  7. Learn Songs with Different Techniques – Practice songs that challenge you with bends, slides, hammer-ons, pull-offs, and fingerstyle.
  8. Practice Alternate Picking – Improve your picking speed and accuracy by alternating between upstrokes and downstrokes.
  9. Improve Left-Hand Dexterity – Practice exercises that improve finger independence and strength, such as chromatic scales or string skipping.
  10. Record Yourself – Listen to your playing and identify areas that need improvement.
